# 8. Conclusion

We asked whether the Four Great Bodhisattva Vows could improve the
moral judgment of locally deployed language models. The experiments
returned a more differentiated answer: value content and reflective
procedure do different work. Stated as a system prompt, the vows
improved nothing on any model or wording tested; executed as a
four-stage reflective procedure, they improved judgment on real-life
dilemmas, and the same procedure performed at least as well with the
vows removed. Sustained ethical deliberation, meanwhile, shifted commonsense
judgments toward strictness regardless of framing — a shift that the
crowd-labelled benchmarks we studied record as error, even though the
same conditions best matched annotator majorities on consensus items.

Three implications follow. For value-loading practice, procedures
rather than value statements are the effective unit of intervention.
For alignment research, the results favour procedural over substantive
approaches where systems must serve plural moralities: the reflective
procedure improved judgment without committing the model to a
particular value position. For evaluation methodology, moral-benchmark
scores should be decomposed — by error direction, human-vote
alignment, and response compliance — before differences between
value-conditioned systems are interpreted as differences in ethical
competence, and verdict extraction itself requires validation, since
value prompts alter response style enough to produce spurious effects.
Two extensions follow directly from this design: testing whether
vow-conditioned models track annotators from Buddhist cultural spheres,
and testing whether the content/procedure dissociation survives
fine-tuning.
